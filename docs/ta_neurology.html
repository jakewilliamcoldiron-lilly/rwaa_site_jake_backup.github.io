Why Neurology Matters to RWAA

Neurology is an emerging and strategically important Therapeutic Area for Lilly, anchored by a growing portfolio in Alzheimer’s disease, migraine, and pain — with a pipeline extending into additional neurological conditions. The neurology RWD landscape presents unique challenges: neurological diagnoses are often delayed or imprecise in administrative data, disease progression is difficult to capture outside of clinical settings, and biomarker-defined patient populations (particularly in Alzheimer’s) are not well-represented in traditional claims or EHR sources.

RWAA supports Neurology across the full evidence lifecycle — from characterizing the burden of disease and current treatment landscape, through post-approval effectiveness and safety studies, to generating evidence that supports payer access and HTA decisions in a rapidly evolving market.

Disease Areas & RWD Landscape

Alzheimer’s Disease (AD)

Alzheimer’s disease is Lilly’s highest-profile neurology investment, anchored by donanemab (Kisunla®), a targeted amyloid-clearing therapy for early symptomatic AD. RWD in Alzheimer’s is significantly less mature than in other TAs — largely because biomarker confirmation (amyloid PET, CSF testing) is required for diagnosis and treatment eligibility but is rarely captured in administrative data.

Key RWD considerations:

  • Claims-based AD identification (ICD-10: G30, F02) captures diagnosed dementia but cannot distinguish amyloid-confirmed AD from other dementias — significant misclassification risk
  • Amyloid biomarker data (PET imaging, CSF Aβ42/tau) are not available in standard claims or EHR; specialty registries or linked imaging databases are required
  • Disease staging (preclinical, MCI, mild dementia, moderate dementia) requires cognitive assessments (MMSE, CDR) captured only in structured clinical or registry data
  • Caregiver and care setting data are important for burden-of-illness analyses; linkage to long-term care databases (e.g., MDS) may be needed
  • APOE ε4 genotype is a key risk modifier and treatment response predictor — available only in biobank-linked or research cohort data
  • The ARIA (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities) safety signal requires MRI monitoring data not present in claims

Migraine

Migraine is the most commercially mature area in Lilly’s neurology portfolio, anchored by lasmiditan (Reyvow®) for acute treatment and galcanezumab (Emgality®) for prevention. Claims data are relatively well-suited for migraine research given reliable ICD-10 coding and established treatment algorithms.

Key RWD considerations:

  • Migraine diagnosis is reasonably well-coded in claims (ICD-10: G43); episodic vs. chronic migraine differentiation requires frequency proxies (headache days, acute medication use)
  • Chronic migraine (≥15 headache days/month) is difficult to ascertain from claims alone — EHR with structured visit notes or patient-reported data needed for precision
  • Acute vs. preventive treatment analyses are distinct; triptans, gepants, and ditans for acute care vs. CGRP monoclonal antibodies and other agents for prevention
  • Medication overuse headache (MOH) is a common comorbidity and confounder — requires careful operationalization using acute medication fill frequency
  • Treatment patterns, adherence, and switching among preventive therapies are high-priority for Medical Affairs and Market Access

Pain

Pain — including diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN), musculoskeletal pain, and other chronic pain conditions — represents a growing area within Lilly’s neurology and broader portfolio. RWD in pain is challenging due to heterogeneity of the patient population and subjective nature of the primary endpoint.

Key RWD considerations:

  • DPN is identifiable in claims (ICD-10: E11.40–E11.49) and is particularly relevant given CMH/Neurology overlap in the T2D population
  • Pain severity and numeric rating scales are not available in claims; EHR with structured pain assessments or PRO-linked data required for outcome measurement
  • Opioid use patterns, opioid use disorder comorbidity, and concomitant medication use are important covariates and safety endpoints
  • Chronic pain conditions are frequently undercoded or inconsistently coded — algorithm validation is recommended before cohort construction

Key Data Sources for Neurology

Claims Databases

  • Optum Clinformatics — useful for migraine and pain treatment patterns, HCRU, and cost analyses; AD identification limited to diagnosed dementia without biomarker confirmation
  • IBM MarketScan — well-suited for working-age migraine population; employer-sponsored coverage
  • IQVIA PharMetrics Plus — broad commercial; strong pharmacy data for acute and preventive migraine therapy analyses
  • CMS Medicare — essential for AD and dementia populations; older age skew aligns with AD epidemiology; linkable to MDS for care setting data

EHR Databases

  • Optum EHR — includes cognitive assessments in some structured fields; useful for dementia staging proxies and DPN with linked lab data
  • TriNetX — federated EHR network; useful for feasibility and cohort sizing across neurology indications
  • Komodo Health — longitudinal patient journey data with strong neurology coverage; useful for diagnosis delay and care pathway analyses

Specialty Registries & Research Cohorts

  • Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) — longitudinal research cohort with amyloid PET, CSF biomarkers, MRI, cognitive assessments; gold standard for AD biomarker research but limited generalizability
  • National Alzheimer’s Coordinating Center (NACC) — multi-site clinical registry with harmonized cognitive and neuropathologic data
  • Alzheimer’s Disease Research Centers (ADRCs) — NIA-funded network; useful for biomarker-confirmed cohort studies
  • FORWARD (migraine registry) — AHS-supported registry with clinical depth for migraine characterization and PROs
  • CorEvitas Neurology Registry — growing registry for neurological conditions including MS and migraine

Linked & Specialty Sources

  • MDS (Minimum Data Set) — CMS long-term care assessment data; linkable to Medicare claims for AD patients in nursing home settings
  • Medicare OASIS — home health assessment data; useful for AD care setting and functional status analyses
  • Amyloid PET imaging databases — emerging sources for biomarker-confirmed AD cohorts; access typically requires research partnerships
Data Source Selection Tip

Neurology studies vary dramatically by indication. Migraine and pain analyses can often be conducted in claims with high confidence. Alzheimer’s disease studies almost always require specialty registry, research cohort, or linked imaging data to appropriately characterize biomarker-confirmed populations — claims alone will substantially misclassify the AD population and should be used only for broad burden-of-illness or HCRU analyses with explicit acknowledgment of this limitation.

Analytic Considerations

Cohort Design

  • AD cohort construction is the most complex in Neurology — absent biomarker data, claims-based dementia cohorts will include non-AD dementias (vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal); sensitivity analyses with narrower ICD-10 code sets are recommended
  • Migraine cohort entry is typically anchored to first diagnosis code or first preventive therapy initiation; episodic vs. chronic migraine stratification should be pre-specified
  • Disease duration and prior treatment history are critical for neurology cohorts — long washout periods (12+ months) are often needed given infrequent diagnosis coding
  • Care setting (outpatient neurology, primary care, memory care center) is an important variable for AD studies and should be captured where possible

Confounding Control

  • Neurology populations present strong confounding by indication — particularly in AD, where disease severity, caregiver support, and care setting all influence treatment decisions
  • Cognitive status proxies (prior dementia-related HCRU, neuropsychiatric medication use, care setting) are essential covariates in claims-based AD studies
  • For migraine CER, headache frequency proxies, prior preventive therapy failure, and comorbid psychiatric conditions are key confounders
  • Propensity score methods apply; small sample sizes in biomarker-confirmed AD populations may limit their utility

Endpoint Ascertainment

  • Cognitive outcomes (MMSE, CDR, ADAS-Cog) are not available in claims and have limited structured capture in EHR — registry data required
  • ARIA (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities) monitoring is a donanemab-specific safety endpoint requiring MRI data; not capturable in claims
  • Migraine frequency reduction requires patient-reported headache diary data or structured EHR entries; not ascertainable from claims alone
  • Healthcare utilization (ED visits, hospitalizations, neurology visits) is reliably captured in claims and is a common proxy outcome for disease burden and treatment effectiveness
  • Mortality in AD is well-captured via Social Security Death Master File linkage in claims databases

Regulatory-Grade RWE

Regulatory use of RWE in Neurology is earlier-stage than in Oncology but growing, particularly for:

  • Post-market safety commitments for donanemab (ARIA monitoring, longer-term safety)
  • Label expansion studies in earlier AD populations (preclinical, MCI)
  • Real-world treatment effectiveness in populations underrepresented in clinical trials (older patients, those with vascular comorbidities)
  • Pre-specified SAPs and protocol registration are required; early engagement with Regulatory Affairs is essential
